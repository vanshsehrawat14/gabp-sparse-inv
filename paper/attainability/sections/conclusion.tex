\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Selected inversion is a classical global operator whose cost is governed by
the elimination fronts. Its log-determinant relationship gives the projected
derivative a reverse schedule with the same asymptotic structural work. On
the finite precision grid in Section~\ref{sec:numerics}, this implementation
has no robust accuracy advantage over a well-ordered dense solve.

The tested hypothesis was that exact structured computation acts as an
inductive bias among models with comparable training fit. In both
pre-registered probes over $30$ held-out seeds, the finite alternative fails
to attain comparable fit in the high-difficulty cells: bounded Jacobi sweeps
in Section~\ref{sec:maze} and finite Neumann adjoints in
Section~\ref{sec:deq}. The diagnostic test-error ratios range from roughly
$600\times$ to $28{,}000\times$, but the matched-fit result attributes these
gaps to attainability, not to a preference among equal-fit solutions. The
DEQ low-difficulty control fails its relative-gap clause. The maze
extrapolation-floor comparison passes, but the separate in-distribution
\texttt{floor\_sound} diagnostic fails, so maze NC2 remains ambiguous. A
later reused-seed analysis is excluded from the evidentiary chain. The
supported conclusion is therefore narrow and hedged: exact structured
computation expands the attainable regime on these controlled, solve-shaped
tasks, while the inductive-bias explanation is not supported.

The limits are explicit: the evidence is at controlled CPU scale and confined
to low-treewidth (1-D and quasi-2-D) problems. Exact selected uncertainty,
implicit gradients near difficult spectra, and certified local-versus-global
computation remain separate questions requiring mature solver baselines and
their own protocols (Section~\ref{sec:program}). The results do not extend
beyond those measured regimes.
