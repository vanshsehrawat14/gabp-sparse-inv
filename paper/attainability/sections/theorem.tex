\subsection{Why it is differentiable at forward cost}
\label{sec:theorem}

For the layer to be usable as a learned component it must be trainable. That it is,
at no asymptotic overhead, is a classical fact, stated once and built on.

\begin{remark}[Selected inversion is a gradient; its adjoint is selected inversion]
\label{rem:scaffold}
Selected inversion is the gradient of the log-determinant restricted to the
pattern:
$\selinv_{\pat}(A) = \nabla_A \log\det A \,|_{\pat}$
\cite{zhu2019sparse,dwyer1948symbolic}. A gradient field has a symmetric
Jacobian, so the reverse-mode adjoint of selected inversion is again a
selected inversion: a structure-preserving two-pass schedule on the same
elimination tree, with no extra fill. Its cost is a constant multiple of the
forward cost by the cheap-gradient principle \cite{baur1983complexity},
i.e.\ $\bigO(\text{fill})$ time and memory, the same asymptotics as the
forward selected inverse. For symmetric $A$ this is literally self-adjoint;
for non-symmetric $A$ the adjoint is selected inversion of $A\T$
(self-adjoint up to transpose), which is why the LU case propagates
independent lower and upper cotangents. The full statement and proof are in
Appendix~\ref{app:theorem}.
\end{remark}

Remark~\ref{rem:scaffold} is why the exact, global, $\bigO(\text{fill})$ solve of
\S\ref{sec:setup} can be used as a layer: embedded in a model and trained
end-to-end at the same cost as the forward pass, never forming the dense
inverse. From here the question is what the exactness of this layer does to
learning.
