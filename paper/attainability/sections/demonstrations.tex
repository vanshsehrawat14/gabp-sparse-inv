\section{Probe 1: exactness buys size extrapolation}
\label{sec:maze}

If exactness is an inductive bias for solve-shaped tasks, its cleanest
signature is size generalization: a model whose global computation is exact at
every size, and whose parameters do not depend on size, should keep working as
the problem grows, while a learner that must approximate the global
computation from finite data should not. A routing task tests this; a
within-architecture control then isolates the cause.

\paragraph{Task and models.}
Each example is a random grid-Laplacian precision $A = L(w) + \varepsilon I$
and a source $s$; the label is the routed field $\inv{A}\,e_s$, an exact,
global functional of the whole graph. A \texttt{gabp} model maps strictly
local per-node inputs $[\phi_v, \deg_v, b_v]$ through a small ($65$-parameter,
size-independent) encoder to an SPD precision, then applies one differentiable
selected-inverse/factorization layer. Two baselines consume the same local inputs: a deep
residual message-passing GNN ($22\,337$ parameters, $344\times$ the capacity)
and a full-attention Transformer with 2-D sinusoidal positions ($18\,305$
parameters, $282\times$; global reach at every layer). Conditioning is handled
by construction: \texttt{softplus} edge weights keep $A$ SPD, and the
$\varepsilon I$ lift pins $\kappa$.

\paragraph{Result: the exact model extrapolates, the approximators collapse.}
Trained at $6\times6$ and evaluated up to $10\times10$ (Table~\ref{tab:maze},
Figure~\ref{fig:maze}), \texttt{gabp} stays at or below $10^{-4}$ at every
size while both baselines degrade monotonically to the predict-the-mean floor
as the diameter grows; per-seed ranges never overlap. With better-trained
($2000$-step) baselines evaluated out of distribution, the gap at
$10\times10$ is roughly $7000\times$ against the GNN and $9000\times$ against
the Transformer. A capacity sweep (width up to roughly $350$k parameters) does not
close the gap; a training-budget sweep ($10\times$ the steps, two learning
rates) narrows the in-distribution gap to about $13\times$ but leaves the
extrapolation gap intact. The paper therefore headlines the training-invariant
axis, size extrapolation, and explicitly does not claim the in-distribution
gap, which is substantially an optimization effect. Sweep records:
\texttt{docs/E4\_BASELINES.md}.

\paragraph{Within-architecture control: the effect is the solve's globality, not the
architecture.}
The baselines match capacity but differ in architecture, so the gap could in
principle be architectural. This paper removes the confound with a within-architecture
intervention: hold the \texttt{gabp} model identical (same encoder, same
parameter count, same data and optimizer) and replace its exact
\texttt{junction\_solve} with $K$ damped-Jacobi sweeps on the same learned
precision $A$. One sweep is one matvec, so $K$ steps reach exactly $K$ hops,
and $K\to\infty$ recovers the exact solve; the intervened variable is the
solve's reach. The dose-response is monotone and decisive
(Table~\ref{tab:maze-causal}, Figure~\ref{fig:maze-causal}): every
bounded-reach variant is worse than predict-the-mean (a truncated solve yields
a confidently wrong near-source field), and only the exact, global solve,
three orders of magnitude below the deepest truncation, succeeds. A
matched-fit check on the held-out seeds (below) shows why: the truncated
arm cannot reach the exact arm's \emph{training} fit at any swept $K$ up to
$256$ (median matched-or-greater capacity gap $\sim2000\times$), so this
dose-response is best read as an attainability/expressivity gap -- a
bounded-reach solve structurally cannot represent a globally-coupled
target -- rather than as evidence that exactness biases training toward a
better solution among equally attainable ones.

\paragraph{Pre-registered matched-fit result on held-out seeds.}
The confirmatory test ran the routed-field task at sizes $6\times6$ to
$48\times48$ on $30$ reserved held-out seeds ($1000$--$1029$), comparing the
exact solve against a compute-matched truncated (Jacobi) baseline within the
same model class, under the frozen rules of
\texttt{archive/docs/PREREGISTRATION\_CONFIRMATORY.md}. The truncated arm is
\textbf{unmatchable for all $30$ seeds}: median exact training loss
$1.45\times10^{-5}$ against the truncated arm's best of $2.96\times10^{-2}$,
a relative gap of about $2000\times$, so no matched-fit regime exists at
any swept budget up to $K=256$ -- confirming the within-architecture result
above is attainability, not a preference among equally-fit models. The
as-trained gap also shrinks with size ($8.17\times10^{-2}$ at size $6$ to
$1.30\times10^{-3}$ at size $48$), so there is no growing-extrapolation
signal beyond the attainability gap itself. Full verdict and per-seed
records: \texttt{archive/docs/CONFIRMATORY\_RESULTS.md} (Maze section),
\texttt{archive/results/CONFIRMATORY/maze\_extrapolation/}.

\paragraph{Not specific to the label.}
The routing label is itself a single solve, so the experiment also regresses the effective
resistance
$R(v,s) = (\inv{A})_{vv} - 2(\inv{A})_{vs} + (\inv{A})_{ss}$, a nonlinear
global functional that additionally needs the diagonal of the inverse. The
same extrapolation signature holds: \texttt{gabp} stays flat at roughly
$2\mathrm{e}{-3}$ from $6\times6$ to $10\times10$, about $40\times$ below the
baselines. The gap is smaller, because the baselines capture some of the
smooth distance structure of $R$, but the shape is the same. The bias is not
an artifact of reconstructing one specific solve. Records:
\texttt{docs/E4\_BASELINES.md}.

\paragraph{Interpretation and scope.}
The task is solve-shaped by construction: the label is the output of a
structured global solve, and the \texttt{gabp} model contains that solve. It
does not learn the solve; it has it. That still explains a real, large,
practical advantage over GNNs and Transformers of far greater capacity. What
it does not establish, on the pre-registered matched-fit evidence above, is
the stronger inductive-bias reading: that among models capable of comparably
fitting the training data, embedding the exact solve is what tips training
toward the generalizing one. The bounded-reach and message-passing
alternatives here are not comparably capable at these budgets; they cannot
fit the target at all in the hard regime, which is an attainability /
expressivity gap, not evidence of an implicit training preference.
Section~\ref{sec:program} states the benchmark-scale version and what would
falsify or refine this reading.

\section{Probe 2: exactness buys trainability at the edge of stability}
\label{sec:deq}

The second axis is stability. A model's optimum often lives where the
underlying fixed point is stiff: long memory, strong coupling, slow mixing.
That is where iterative gradient estimates fail. This section shows that the
exact selected-inverse backward keeps gradients correct in that regime, so it
can train where iterative implicit differentiation cannot.

\paragraph{The operator's role.}
A deep-equilibrium layer defines its output as a fixed point
$z^\star = f(z^\star, x)$. The implicit function theorem makes its backward
pass a single non-symmetric solve with the equilibrium Jacobian
$J=\partial f/\partial z$,
\begin{equation}
  (I-J)\T u = \partial\loss/\partial z^\star,
  \qquad \partial\loss/\partial\theta = u\T \,\partial f/\partial\theta .
  \label{eq:ift}
\end{equation}
When the coupling of $f$ is sparse on a low-treewidth graph, which is this
paper's regime, \eqref{eq:ift} is exactly the non-symmetric junction solve
sibling of selected inversion on $A = I-J$: one block $LDU$ factorization at
$\bigO(\text{fill})$,
with the transpose of the same factors giving the adjoint at no extra cost
(Remark~\ref{rem:scaffold}). Standard DEQ training instead solves
\eqref{eq:ift} iteratively ($u_{k+1} = J\T u_k + g$), which converges like
$\rho(J)^K$, hence arbitrarily slowly as the fixed point stiffens and
$\rho(J)\to1$.

\paragraph{Result: exact at every $\rho$; iterative degrades.}
On a non-symmetric coupling on a loopy $3\times3$ grid (genuine fill) scaled
to a target spectral radius, the experiment measures the parameter-gradient relative error
against a dense implicit-differentiation oracle (Table~\ref{tab:deq},
Figure~\ref{fig:deq}). The exact selected-inverse backward is at machine
precision throughout; the $1\mathrm{e}{-12}$ floor at $\rho=0.999$ is the
$\kappa u$ of the nearly singular solve, not iteration error. The iterative
backward tracks $\rho^K$: at $\rho=0.99$ even $32$ iterations leave the
gradient $77\%$ wrong, and at $\rho=0.999$ it is essentially $100\%$ wrong. A
linear layer trains cleanly through the exact backward (MSE from about $11$
to about $10^{-11}$), and the nonlinear implicit-function-theorem backward is
validated against autograd through a fully unrolled solver to near machine
precision.

\paragraph{Confirmatory replication on held-out seeds: matched-fit result.}
The gradient-error measurement above is exploratory, so a confirmatory,
training-level version of the claim was pre-registered and run once on $30$
reserved held-out seeds ($1000$--$1029$) that were never touched during
development. Both arms train a scalar gain through the same exact forward
solve ($n=16$, non-symmetric coupling), held bit-identical between arms; the
only intervention is the backward rule used during training, the exact
implicit adjoint versus a biased $K$-term Neumann adjoint. An initial,
cruder analysis (a paired-bootstrap test of the held-out loss difference
alone) clears a fixed effect-size bar at the three primary cells
($\rho=0.99$, $K\in\{1,2,4\}$) and stays silent on the low-$\rho$ controls --
so there \emph{is} a reliable, reproducible difference in what the two arms
learn. But that test alone cannot distinguish two mechanisms: an inductive
bias (both arms reach comparable training fit; exactness tips training
toward the better-generalizing optimum) versus attainability (the Neumann
arm cannot reach comparable training fit at all, so of course it
generalizes worse). The pre-registered matched-fit adjudication
(\texttt{archive/bench/matched\_fit\_taskA.py}) is designed
exactly to separate them, by checking whether the Neumann arm can match the
exact arm's training loss (gated by a frozen floor and a $60\%$ matchability
bar) before crediting any test-loss gap to an inductive-bias mechanism. At
the primary cells the Neumann arm is matchable in only $0$--$23\%$ of seeds
(relative train gap $+3.9$ to $+13.2$), so the inductive-bias
(\textsc{survive}) branch does not fire; the result resolves to the
conservative attainability/optimization reading. The one designed silent
control ($\rho=0.5$) did not cleanly pass: its relative-gap clause failed
(driven by near-machine-precision instability in the ratio, both arms
converging to losses of order $10^{-8}$--$10^{-9}$), though its
independent function-distance clause passed by more than four orders of
magnitude. Per the frozen rule (a positive reading is void unless its
negative controls hold), this is reported as a hedged, not pristine,
conservative verdict, not relaxed after the fact. Full adjudication, per-cell
table, and per-seed records:
\texttt{archive/docs/CONFIRMATORY\_RESULTS.md},
\texttt{archive/docs/PREREGISTRATION\_CONFIRMATORY.md},
\texttt{archive/results/CONFIRMATORY/}.

\paragraph{Interpretation and scope.}
The error sweep is a mechanism result at controlled scale ($n=9$): it shows
the exact $\bigO(\text{fill})$ backward is correct where the iterative one is
not, and that this gradient-level fact propagates to a real, reproducible
training-level difference. What the matched-fit adjudication above rules out
is the stronger, specific claim that this difference reflects an inductive
bias among equally-attainable optima; on this evidence it instead reflects
the iterative arm's inability to fit the stiff cells at all, which is an
attainability/optimization fact about biased gradients, not a preference
effect. Neumann iteration is the transparent baseline; accelerated solvers
(Anderson~\cite{anderson1965iterative},
Broyden~\cite{broyden1965class}) reduce the iteration count but not the
$\rho\to1$ scaling, while the structured solve removes it. The analysis assumes $\rho(J)<1$ (a
contraction), and the advantage is for graph-structured, low-treewidth
Jacobians; for a dense $J$ the $LDU$ factorization is $\bigO(n^3)$ and
iteration is the right tool. The stronger claim, that this lets one train
into a stiff, long-range regime at benchmark scale for a learned-capability
gain, is the benchmark-scale experiment E4 of Section~\ref{sec:program}, not
yet demonstrated, and would need its own matched-fit or attainability control
to attribute correctly.
